## Why the US and India views are deliberately asymmetric
CurveIQ builds the full analytic toolkit for the US and a deliberately smaller subset
for India. This is a data-availability decision, not an oversight. Free US data
(FRED) provides a complete daily curve across eleven tenors, real yields and
breakevens from the TIPS market, official FRED-computed spreads, and NBER recession
dates. Free India data provides a monthly 10-year benchmark and a short rate, and
little else: there is no free full curve (the CCIL curve is paid), no liquid inflation-
linked market to derive breakevens, and no official recession-dating body. So the
India view honestly shows fewer panels, each missing one annotated with why.

## Why the US recession signal does not transfer to India
The inverted-curve-precedes-recession regularity is a US (and developed-market)
phenomenon that does not port cleanly to India. Several structural reasons: a large
share of Indian government bonds is held by captive domestic institutions under
statutory requirements, so yields reflect regulated demand as much as free-market
expectations; the curve is shorter and less liquid; capital flows and the currency
transmit external shocks that swamp domestic-cycle signals; and inflation dynamics
differ. The result is that Indian curve inversions are rare and carry little of the
recession-forecasting content they have in the US. Showing the India view as a smaller,
caveated companion is the way to represent this faithfully.

## Descriptive, not prescriptive
CurveIQ is strictly descriptive and retrospective. It computes and explains what the
term structure, spreads, regimes, and equity–yield relationship have been; it never
tells a user what to do, what to buy, or what will happen. Any narration is grounded in
the computed numbers and the curated corpus, and forward-looking or prescriptive
phrasing is treated as a defect to be blocked.
