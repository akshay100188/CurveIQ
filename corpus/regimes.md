## Recession regimes and crisis shading
A regime overlay marks periods of economic stress on a chart so movements can be read
in context. For the US, the National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) publishes
official recession start and end dates after the fact; these are drop-in shading and
require no judgement on our part. They are retrospective by construction — NBER dates
a recession months or years after it began.

## Why the policy rate is shown separately from market yields
The central bank's policy rate (the fed funds target range in the US, the repo rate in
India) is set administratively, not discovered in a market. Plotting it on the same
"market movement" axis as a traded yield would imply it moves for the same reasons,
which is false. The discipline here is to tag every series as either market or
administered and never render an administered rate as if it were a market move; it can
be shown alongside, as context, but on its own footing.

## Reading the equity–yield relationship honestly
The correlation between equity returns and yield changes is regime-dependent and
unstable. Reported as a single number over a long sample it averages out to something
small and near zero, which hides the structure. The honest presentation splits the
correlation by regime — inside versus outside stress windows — where the sign and
magnitude often differ sharply. A lone average would erase exactly the information a
reader needs; the regime split is the point, not a footnote.
