## US 2019 inversion
Through 2019 the US curve inverted: the 10-year fell below the 3-month from May and
the 10Y–2Y briefly turned negative in late August. Descriptively, the market was
pricing the Fed as tight relative to the expected path of future short rates, after a
2018 hiking cycle. The classifier flags 2019 as inverted on the 10Y–3M anchor even
though the 10Y–2Y only grazed zero, because the 3-month signal inverted earlier and
deeper. A recession did follow in 2020, though its proximate cause (a pandemic) was
unrelated to what the curve had been signalling.

## India Taper Tantrum, 2013
In mid-2013 the US Federal Reserve signalled it would taper asset purchases. Capital
flowed out of emerging markets; the Indian rupee fell sharply and Indian bond yields
spiked. This is shaded as a crisis window (roughly May to September 2013). It is a
useful contrast to the US case: the stress originated abroad and transmitted through
the currency and capital flows, not through a domestic curve inversion. India's curve
does not carry the same recession-signalling content as the US curve.

## India COVID, 2020
The COVID shock in early 2020 (shaded roughly February to April) hit India through a
collapse in activity and a flight to safety. The RBI cut the repo rate and yields
fell. Because India has no official recession-dating body analogous to the NBER, these
crisis windows are hard-coded constants rather than a sourced series — an honest
acknowledgement of a data limit, not an estimate dressed up as data.
