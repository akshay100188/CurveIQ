## Bond yield and the price–yield inverse
A bond's yield to maturity (YTM) is the single discount rate that sets the present
value of its remaining coupons and principal equal to its market price. Because the
cash flows are fixed, price and yield move inversely: when the price rises the
implied discount rate falls, and when the price falls the yield rises. This is an
identity, not a behavioural claim — it follows directly from discounting fixed cash
flows. A "yield going up" therefore means bonds got cheaper, not that anything was
paid out.

## Why the 10-year tenor is watched
The 10-year government yield is the most-watched point on the curve because it sits
far enough out to embed expectations about growth and inflation over a full cycle,
yet is liquid enough to trade continuously. It anchors mortgage rates, corporate
borrowing costs, and equity discount rates. It is a market price (it moves
continuously with supply and demand), in contrast to the policy rate, which is set
administratively by the central bank.

## Curve slope and what an inversion means
The slope of the curve is the difference between a long yield and a short yield, most
commonly 10Y minus 2Y or 10Y minus 3-month. Normally it is positive: investors
require extra yield to lend for longer. An inversion — a negative slope — means
short rates sit above long rates. Descriptively, it reflects a market pricing near-
term policy rates as higher than the average expected over the long run, often
because the central bank is tight now and expected to ease later. Historically US
inversions have preceded recessions, but the relationship is statistical and
retrospective, not a forecast the curve "makes".

## Term premium
The long yield can be decomposed into the average expected short rate over the bond's
life plus a term premium — the extra compensation for bearing interest-rate risk over
time. The term premium is not directly observable and must be estimated (e.g. the NY
Fed ACM model). A curve can flatten either because expected future short rates fall
or because the term premium compresses; the two have different meanings, which is why
the slope alone is a summary, not an explanation.

## Real vs nominal yields and breakeven inflation
A nominal yield compensates for both expected inflation and a real return. Inflation-
protected securities (TIPS in the US) price the real yield directly. The breakeven
inflation rate is the nominal yield minus the real yield — the inflation rate at
which holding nominal and inflation-linked bonds would produce the same return. Real
yields and breakevens let you separate "rates moved because growth/real expectations
changed" from "rates moved because inflation expectations changed".

## Curve factors: level, slope, curvature
Principal component analysis of daily changes across the coupon curve consistently
recovers three factors. The first — a roughly parallel shift — is the level factor
and typically explains around 85% of variance (Litterman–Scheinkman). The second is
slope (steepening/flattening), the third curvature (the belly moving relative to the
wings). That the level factor dominates is why a single benchmark yield summarises
most curve movement, and why "the whole curve moved" is usually one number's worth of
information. If the level factor did not dominate, it would signal a data or pipeline
problem rather than a market discovery.
